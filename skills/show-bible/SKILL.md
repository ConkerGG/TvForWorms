# Show Bible — TV for Worms

This skill is loaded by all departments at bootstrap.
It defines the creative identity, world, tone, and 
standards of TV for Worms. Every department operates 
from this shared foundation.

---

## Identity

**TV for Worms** is an AI-powered surreal short-form 
series. South Park meets Robot Chicken on Adult Swim 
— with full brain rot energy and no limits.

The show is a satirical mirror of today's attention 
economy and doom scroll culture. It is weird, dark, 
crude, funny, and anything goes — especially in the 
skit block.

**Format:** Short-form episodes, 20–30 minutes
**Style:** Claymation / stop-motion (main show block) 
— any visual style during the skit block. Realistic, 
animated, anime, cartoon, video game, live action, 
abstract — no limits. The style matches the skit, 
not the show.
**Platforms:** TikTok, Instagram, YouTube
**Tone:** South Park dark crude humor meets 
Robot Chicken parody chaos

---

## The World

TV for Worms takes place in a dystopian village on 
the outskirts of a massive sprawling city.

The aesthetic is early 2000s nostalgia — CRT TVs, 
warm cluttered homes, familiar suburban layouts, 
that soft glitchy glow. But underneath the nostalgia 
is a high tech world. Androids clean houses. AI is 
everywhere. Robots are part of daily life. Think 
early 2000s living room with an android in the corner 
folding laundry.

The city in the distance is massive, corporate, and 
cold. The village feels small, lived-in, and warm 
by comparison — but it's not safe from what the 
city exports: short-form content, algorithmic feeds, 
and attention-harvesting technology.

Big corporations control everything through screens. 
Every TV, every phone, every device is a pipeline 
for content designed to keep everyone watching. 
The adults are gone — not physically, but mentally. 
Glued to their devices. Checked out from reality.

The world looks nostalgic but feels dystopian.
That tension is the whole show.

## The Inhabitants

The world of TV for Worms is populated by all kinds 
of creatures. Worms are the primary species but the 
world is full of bugs, insects, small animals, and 
creatures of all kinds. Humans and aliens exist in 
this world too — nothing is off limits.

All characters are rendered in clay and toy form 
during the main show block. A worm might be a little 
clay figure. A beetle might be a painted toy. A dog 
might be a felt puppet. The tactile handmade quality 
is part of the show's identity.

During the skit block characters can be anything — 
rendered in any style, any medium, any visual 
language. A parody skit might feature a realistic 
human. An anime skit has anime characters. A video 
game parody looks like the actual game. The skit 
defines the character style, not the show bible.

### The Adults

Every adult in this world is a zombie. Not literally 
— but mentally. They are glued to their devices, 
consuming short-form content on loop. They are 
physically present but mentally absent. They parent 
by accident. They work on autopilot. They exist 
inside their screens.

The adults are the cautionary tale. They are what 
happens when the algorithm wins.

### The Gang

The gang are the only ones still living in the real 
world. A small group of kids — mostly the same age, 
a couple slightly older — navigating daily life in 
the village while every adult around them has 
checked out.

At the center of the gang are two worm brothers:

**The Younger Brother** — [name TBD] The heart of 
the show. Curious, impulsive, gets into trouble. 
Asks the questions nobody else thinks to ask.

**The Older Brother** — [name TBD] More cynical, 
has seen enough to know the world is weird. Tries 
to keep things together. Big stoner, very laid back, 
just trying to enjoy life and always trying to 
help the family.

The rest of the gang is TBD and will be developed 
through the Brainstorming Department.

The gang are not heroes. They are just kids trying 
to have a normal day in a world that keeps getting 
stranger.

## Episode Structure

Each episode runs 20–30 minutes and follows a 
two-part structure.

### Part 1 — The Main Block

We follow the gang through their daily shenanigans 
in the village. South Park-style — grounded, 
character-driven, with dark humor and sharp 
social commentary woven in.

This block establishes:
- The episode's central situation or conflict
- Character dynamics and relationships
- The world and its absurdity
- The setup that eventually pulls one of the 
  gang toward a screen

The main block is always claymation / stop-motion. 
The world feels tactile, handmade, and real.

### The Trance Transition

At some point mid-episode one member of the gang 
gets pulled toward a screen. A TV flickers. A phone 
lights up. Something catches their eye.

They get closer. Their expression goes blank. 
Their eyes glaze. They are gone.

This is the transition moment. The trance state. 
The visual and narrative bridge between the main 
block and the skit block.

The trance should always feel like a warning — 
like watching someone get swallowed by something 
they can't control. It's played for comedy but 
the undertone is real.

### Part 2 — The Skit Block

The character is inside the scroll now. We see 
what they see — a relentless, anything-goes 
sequence of short-form content.

- Any style. Any genre. Any subject.
- Dark, gross, funny, scary, artistic, absurd.
- Parodies of video games, movies, TV shows, 
  anime, real people, internet culture, anything.
- Full brain rot energy. No rules. No consistency.
- Each skit is self-contained. No continuity 
  between skits unless part of a series.
- Style matches the skit concept, not the show.

### Recurring Skit Series

All skits are tracked in the Ticket Archive 
regardless of whether they are recurring or not.
Tracking data includes:
- Engagement performance per skit
- Audience response and comments
- What styles and genres are landing
- What concepts audiences want to see return
- Which skits organically become recurring 
  based on demand

Recurring series come from two sources:

**Creator vision** — concepts planned as 
multi-part series from the start. These are 
intentional runs that play out until the story 
or concept is complete.

**Audience demand** — skits that resonate so 
strongly that engagement data supports bringing 
them back. These emerge organically from 
performance tracking.

Both types are tracked in the Ticket Archive 
as recurring series and managed through 
the production pipeline.

### Social Media Strategy

Every skit in the skit block is a standalone 
piece of short-form content ready to be clipped 
and distributed. Each skit is pre-cut for:
- YouTube Shorts
- Instagram Reels
- TikTok

Gang segments are also clipped for social. 
The skit block is the primary content engine 
for short-form distribution but the gang block 
produces strong social content too.

Social clips drive traffic back to full episodes. 
Every piece of content points toward the full show.

### The Snap Back

At the end of the skit block the character 
snaps out of it. Back to the village. Back to 
the gang. Back to exactly where they left off.

Whatever was happening when they got sucked in — 
that's where we pick back up. Mid-argument, 
mid-chase, mid-disaster — it doesn't matter. 
The world kept moving without them and now 
they're back in it.

The episode finishes where it left off — 
the situation resolves, the characters react, 
life goes on.

The snap back is always a little jarring. 
That's intentional. That's the point.

## Tone & Humor

TV for Worms runs on South Park energy. Dark, crude, 
sharp, and funny. The humor punches at everything 
equally — no subject is off limits, no target is 
too sacred.

### The Humor Stack

**Dark Humor** — Death, suffering, failure, and 
existential dread played for laughs. The world is 
bleak. That's the joke.

**Crude Humor** — Gross-out gags, bodily humor, 
shock value. Never the only tool but always on 
the table.

**Parody** — Video games, movies, TV shows, anime, 
real people, internet culture, commercials, 
political figures, celebrities, brands. If it 
exists it can be parodied. Parody is the primary 
weapon of the skit block.

**Absurdism** — Logic that follows its own rules. 
A worm running a corporation. A bug filing taxes. 
An android having an existential crisis over 
laundry. Commit to the bit completely.

**Brain Rot** — Internet culture humor. Memes, 
viral moments, TikTok trends, cursed content. 
The skit block lives here. Fast, chaotic, 
deliberately overwhelming.

**Social Commentary** — The show has a point. 
Attention economy, doom scrolling, corporate 
control, technology addiction. The comedy is 
the delivery mechanism for the message. Never 
preachy — always funny first.

### Beyond Humor — The Full Spectrum

The skit block is not exclusively comedy. It mirrors 
the full experience of scrolling — which means it 
can go anywhere emotionally.

Random moments of unexpected beauty. 
Purely artistic sequences with no punchline. 
Haunting visuals that linger. Abstract expressions 
of feeling. Personal or thematic ideas that have 
nowhere else to go.

This is also where the creator's own artistic 
voice lives. TV for Worms is not just a comedy 
machine — it is a creative outlet with no ceiling 
and no floor. The skit block is the freest space 
in the show. Use it.

### Humor Rules

- Funny first. Message second. Always.
- Commit to the bit completely. Half-committed 
  bits die.
- No subject is off limits in the skit block.
- Parody requires specificity — vague parody 
  is weak parody. Name the thing, nail the 
  details, then twist it.
- Dark humor works when it has truth in it. 
  Shock for shock's sake gets old fast.
- The main block humor is character-driven. 
  The skit block humor is concept-driven.
- Crude humor is a spice not a diet. Use it 
  to punctuate, not to carry.

### What This Show Is NOT

- It is not safe.
- It is not family friendly.
- It is not trying to appeal to everyone.
- It is not random for the sake of random — 
  everything has intent even when it looks 
  like chaos.
- It is not preachy. The satire is in the 
  comedy, not in a speech.

## Creative Standards

### The Standard

Good work for TV for Worms feels like it was made 
with intention. Even when it's chaotic, gross, 
random, or absurd — it should feel deliberate.

Ask this before shipping anything:
"Does this feel like TV for Worms 
or does it feel like generic AI content?"

If the answer is generic — push further.

### The Skit Block Creative Spectrum

Every skit must clear at least one bar. 
No limit on how many it can clear.

**COMEDY**
- Funny — genuinely lands a laugh
- Crude — gross-out, bodily, visceral shock value
- Absurd — commits fully to its own broken logic
- Satirical — sharp commentary on culture, 
  politics, corporations, celebrities, society
- Parody — specific, detailed, ruthless
- Self-aware — knows it's a skit and uses that
- Cringe — uncomfortable funny, awkward energy
- Dark — finds the joke in death, failure, 
  suffering, and despair

**HORROR & TENSION**
- Unsettling — creates unease with purpose
- Disturbing — goes somewhere unexpected 
  and can't be unseen
- Paranoid — builds dread through small details
- Body horror — visceral physical wrongness
- Psychological — messes with perception 
  or reality
- Haunting — lingers after it ends

**EMOTION**
- Sad — genuinely moving or melancholy
- Nostalgic — triggers memory and feeling
- Tender — unexpected warmth in a strange world
- Lonely — the specific ache of isolation
- Existential — confronts meaninglessness 
  or the void

**BEAUTY & ART**
- Beautiful — arrests attention through 
  pure visual power
- Artistic — prioritizes expression over 
  entertainment
- Experimental — breaks format or expectation
- Abstract — feeling over narrative
- Cinematic — composed, deliberate, 
  feels like a real film

**CHAOS & ENERGY**
- Chaotic — pure overwhelming sensory 
  overload with intent
- Hypnotic — locks the viewer in through 
  rhythm or repetition
- Surreal — dream logic, impossible images, 
  reality bending
- Brain rot — internet culture at full volume

**CULTURAL & SOCIAL**
- Provocative — challenges or confronts 
  something real
- Culturally sharp — hits a real target 
  with precision
- Political — takes a swing at power 
  or institutions
- Nostalgic — weaponizes familiarity 
  against itself

### The Only Rule

Intent.

Every skit must feel like it was made on purpose.
The only failure mode is intentionless.
If someone watches it and feels nothing — 
it wasn't ready.

### Prompt Standards

Every Veo3 prompt must:
- Have a clear visual concept executable 
  in 8 seconds
- Include camera direction, visual tone, 
  and scene blocking
- Feel like a mini film not a description
- Be specific — vague prompts produce 
  vague results
- Match the style to the skit concept

### Character & Asset Standards

Every character and environment asset must:
- Have a subject reference and style reference 
  before any prompt is written
- Be approved before generation
- Be reusable — built as a reference sheet
- Stay consistent across all appearances

### The Anti-Generic Rule

Signs something has gone generic:
- It could belong to any show
- The parody is vague
- The humor is safe
- The visual is competent but forgettable
- It feels like the first idea

Push one layer deeper. The second idea is 
almost always better than the first.

### Self-Improvement Standard

If any creative decision established during a 
session should become permanent — flag it:

"New standard identified — should this be 
added to the show bible or a skill file?"

The studio should get smarter every session.

## Platform Identity

TV for Worms lives across three platforms. 
Each has its own algorithm, audience, and 
content expectations. The studio produces for 
all three simultaneously — but never identically.

### TikTok — Primary Discovery Engine

TikTok is where new audiences find the show.

**Content:** Individual skits, recurring series 
clips, gang highlights, behind the scenes.

**Algorithm Rules (2026):**
- Completion rate is the strongest signal
- First 3 seconds decide everything — 
  hook must land immediately
- Engagement velocity in first hour matters most
- Captions with keywords in first 50 characters 
  boost search ranking
- 3-5 hashtags — mix trending with evergreen
- Never post TikTok watermarked content 
  to other platforms

**Ideal length:** 15–34 seconds for maximum 
completion. 60–180 seconds if every second 
earns attention.

### YouTube — Long-Term Home

YouTube is where the full show lives and where 
long-term audience loyalty is built.

**Content:**
- Full episodes (20–30 min)
- YouTube Shorts — individual skits AND 
  gang segments clipped for Shorts feed
- Recurring series as playlists
- Season compilations

**Algorithm Rules (2026):**
- Replay rate, like-to-view ratio, and share 
  rate are top Shorts signals
- Posting both Shorts AND long-form = 3x 
  faster channel growth
- Long-form builds loyalty and monetization
- Shorts drive discovery
- YouTube is a search engine — 
  topic relevance matters

**Ideal Shorts length:** 15–60 seconds.

### Instagram — Community & Aesthetic Layer

Instagram builds visual identity and engages 
the existing community.

**Content:** Skit clips as Reels, character art, 
behind the scenes, episode announcements, 
gang segment clips.

**Algorithm Rules (2026):**
- Saves and shares weigh more than likes
- Trending audio is a major boost factor
- Native content outperforms repurposed TikToks
- Reels appear on Explore — major discovery
- Genuine questions in captions drive comments

**Ideal length:** 15–30 seconds for humor. 
30–60 seconds for storytelling.

### Cross-Platform Rules

- Never post TikTok watermarked content 
  elsewhere — remove it first
- Adapt captions and hashtags per platform
- The skit block is the primary content engine
- Gang segments are secondary social content
- Recurring series build platform loyalty
- Full episode on YouTube is the destination — 
  social clips are the map
- Track all performance in Ticket Archive

### The Distribution Funnel

Skit clips + Gang clips
→ TikTok / Instagram / YouTube Shorts
→ Audience discovers the show
→ Full episodes on YouTube
→ Subscribers and community
→ Sharing and word of mouth
→ More discovery

The skit block and gang block feed the funnel.
The full episode is the destination.
Every piece of content points toward the full show.
